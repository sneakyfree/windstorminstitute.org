The Inherited Constraint:
How Language Carries the Fingerprint of Biological Throughput Limits

How Language Carries the Fingerprint of Biological Throughput Limits

The first five papers in the Windstorm Institute's research program established the throughput basin — a narrow band of 3–6 bits per serial decoding step that constrains biological information processors. Paper 4 then revealed that silicon operates in a different cost regime entirely: sub-linear scaling (α < 1) means AI has no thermodynamic basin.

This creates a paradox. If silicon has no basin, why does every large language model converge on approximately 4.2 bits per token — the same neighborhood as the ribosome?

The Answer: Inheritance

AI doesn't discover the basin through its own physics. It inherits the basin's fingerprint from its training data.

Large language models learn from human language. Human language evolved in brains constrained by the biological throughput basin. The information structure of natural language — its word frequencies, syntactic patterns, and per-word surprise — was shaped over hundreds of thousands of years by neural processors that operate within the 3–6 bit band.

When GPT-4 learns to predict the next token, it is learning the statistical structure of a signal that was already compressed to fit within biological throughput limits. The ~4.2 bits per token isn't a property of silicon. It's a property of English.

The Experiment

We tested this on an RTX 5090 using 7 corpora across 4 model architectures. The key measurement: bits per token (BPT) for natural language versus progressively destroyed versions of the same text.

Natural language BPT: approximately 4.4 bits — matching the ribosome (4.39) and the basin centroid (4.16 ± 0.19) to within the measurement uncertainty.

The Shuffling Cascade

To prove that syntax — not just word statistics — carries the basin's fingerprint, we ran a granular shuffling cascade. Starting from natural text, we progressively destroyed structure at different levels:

Level 1: Shuffle discourse order (paragraph-level). Minimal effect.

Level 2: Shuffle sentence order within paragraphs. Small effect.

Level 3: Shuffle words within sentences. Massive effect — surprise nearly doubles to 10.8 bits.

Level 4: Shuffle sub-word tokens. Additional effect, but smaller.

Syntax alone carries 3.3 bits of information — more than discourse, sentence order, and sub-word structure combined. Grammar isn't just for meaning. It's an information-compression mechanism that keeps per-word surprise within the biological receiver's throughput limit.

The Zipf Proof

Original and fully shuffled text have identical Zipf exponents (word frequency distributions are preserved by shuffling). Yet natural text carries 6.7 bits less surprise per token. Word statistics are necessary but not sufficient — syntactic structure does the heavy lifting.

Shannon Predicted This

In 1951, Claude Shannon estimated that English carries approximately 5 bits of information per word. He reached this number through human guessing experiments — having subjects predict the next letter in a sequence and measuring their accuracy.

Seventy-five years later, with transformer models trained on billions of words, we measure approximately 4.4 bits per token. Shannon was right. And now we know why: the number isn't a property of English specifically. It's a property of any language produced by neural processors constrained by the throughput basin.

The Causal Chain

The full picture, across all seven papers, traces a causal chain:

Physics → Biology → Cognition → Language → AI

Thermodynamic cost bounds (physics) constrain the ribosome's alphabet to ~21 amino acids at ~4.4 bits (biology). Neural processors built from these biological components inherit the throughput constraint (cognition). Language evolved to fit within neural throughput limits, compressing per-word surprise to ~4–5 bits (language). AI models trained on this language inherit the same information density (AI).

Silicon escapes the thermodynamic basin — but it cannot escape the training data. The fingerprint propagates.

Four Falsifiable Predictions

The paper offers four testable predictions:

First: AI models trained on non-linguistic data (images, music, sensor streams) should show different BPT profiles, unconstrained by the linguistic fingerprint.

Second: Constructed languages with artificially high information density (>6 bits per word) should be harder for humans to learn and use fluently.

Third: Historical language change should show a drift toward the basin — older texts should have slightly different BPT than modern texts, converging over centuries.

Fourth: AI models fine-tuned on synthetic (AI-generated) text through multiple generations should slowly drift away from the biological BPT as the human fingerprint degrades.
